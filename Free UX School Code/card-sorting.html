What is Card Sorting?

By: John James

Approximate read time: 6 min

Overview

Card sorting is a user-centered design technique that is used to help designers understand how users think about and organize information. It involves giving users a set of cards, each containing a piece of content or a label, and asking them to sort the cards into categories or groups that make sense to them. Card sorting can be conducted online or in person, and can be used to design website navigation, information architecture, and content organization.

Why Card Sorting is Essential in UX

Card sorting is essential to the UX research and architecture process because it helps designers to understand the mental models and organizing principles that users use to make sense of information. By observing how users sort the cards, designers can gain insight into the categories, labels, and relationships that are most meaningful and intuitive to the user. This information can then be used to design a user-centered information architecture that reflects the user's perspective and needs.

Three Types of Card Sorting That Can Be Used in UX

There are several types of card sorting that can be used in the UX research and architecture process:
  1. Open card sorting: In open card sorting, users are asked to create their own categories and labels for the cards. This allows designers to understand how users naturally think about and organize the content, and can help to identify common themes and patterns.
  2. Closed card sorting: In closed card sorting, users are asked to sort the cards into pre- defined categories and labels. This can be useful for testing and validating existing information architectures, or for comparing the user's perspective with that of the designer or business.
  3. Hybrid card sorting: Hybrid card sorting combines elements of open and closed card sorting. Users may be asked to create some categories and labels, and to sort the remaining cards into pre-defined categories. This can be useful for combining the insights of open and closed card sorting, or for testing specific hypotheses about the user's mental model.

Best Practices You Should Implement When Conducting a Card Sort

Card sorting can be a valuable tool for UX researchers and architects, as it provides a direct and concrete way to gather data and insights about the user's perspective. It is particularly useful for understanding the relationships and hierarchies between different pieces of content or labels, and for identifying gaps or redundancies in the information architecture.

There are several best practices that UX researchers and architects can follow to ensure that card sorting is conducted effectively:
  1. Use a diverse and representative sample of users: To get a rich and accurate understanding of the user's perspective, it is important to include a diverse and representative sample of users in the card sorting study. This may include users from different age groups, cultural backgrounds, or technical skill levels.
  2. Use clear and concise cards: To minimize confusion and ensure that the results are meaningful, it is important to use clear and concise cards that are easy for users to understand and categorize.
  3. Use a consistent and unbiased sorting process: To ensure that the results are accurate and comparable, it is important to use a consistent and unbiased sorting process across all users. This may involve using standardized instructions, sorting criteria, or categorization rules.
  4. Analyze and interpret the results carefully: To extract meaningful insights from the card sorting data, it is important to analyze and interpret the results carefully. This may involve using tools such as affinity diagrams or statistical analysis to identify patterns and trends in the data.

Conclusion

Overall, card sorting is a valuable and effective tool for UX researchers and architects. By using card sorting to understand the user's mental models and organizing principles, designers can create a user-centered information architecture that reflects the needs and perspectives of the user.
