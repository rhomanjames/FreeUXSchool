What are Deliverables?

By: John James

Approximate read time: 4 min

Overview

In the UX design process, deliverables are the products or outcomes that are produced at various stages of the process and that are delivered to stakeholders or clients. Deliverables can include prototypes, wireframes, user flows, design mockups, user research reports, and other documents or artifacts that are used to communicate and document the design of the product or service.

Why Are Deliverables Essential in UX Design?

There are several reasons why deliverables are essential in the UX design process:

They help to communicate and document the design: Deliverables serve as a way to communicate and document the design of the product or service. They can be used to present and explain the design to stakeholders or clients, and to provide a record of the design decisions and processes that were followed. This can help to ensure that the design is understood and agreed upon by all parties, and can help to avoid misunderstandings or miscommunications.

They help to validate and iterate the design: Deliverables can also be used to validate and iterate the design. By presenting the design to users or stakeholders and gathering feedback, designers can identify and address issues or problems with the design, and can make improvements to the user experience. This can help to ensure that the design meets the needs and goals of the user and the business.

They help to manage the design process: Deliverables can also be used to manage the design process. By defining the scope and objectives of each deliverable, and by establishing a timeline for their completion, designers can help to ensure that the design process stays on track and that the product or service is delivered on time.

How to Make Effective Deliverables

To be effective, deliverables should be carefully planned and designed, and should be tailored to the needs and goals of the project and the stakeholders. They should be clearly and concisely written or presented, and should be easy to understand and interpret. They should also be aligned with the overall design strategy and goals of the product or service, and should be iterated and refined as needed based on feedback and user testing.
