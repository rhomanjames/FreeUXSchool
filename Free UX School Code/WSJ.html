about > WSJ Redesign

Overview

I served as the UX Architecture lead on team of three to explore a new concept for redesigning the Wall Street Journal.

Challenge

Purpose

I had been working at Dow Jones for a few months before starting this project. I was primarily focused with one of Dow Jones' subsidiaries, MarketWatch.com, where I lead the UX strategy and architecture.

The VP of UX architecture informed me that I would be a part of an initiative to explore redesign concept for the Wall Street Journal (WSJ), the most popular of the Dow Jones brands. WSJ has had a similar look for decades, so proposing an entire structural and design makeover that could affect the future of the brand was truly a monumental task, but also an honor.

To kickstart the process, the design executives brought together designers and architects from across the brand to visually hypothesize the future of the Wall Street Journal.

Scope

WSJ reaches over 42 million digital readers per month, which positions them as one of the top digital news sources in the world. Though it would be admirable for a team of three to single-handedly redesign this experience, we all know this would be imposisble.

For the scope of this project, we just focused on redesigning the home page experience to explore the possibilities of what WSJ could evolve into. WSJ has been around for over 120 years, with their digital age nearing 30. With such prestige and history at stake, the importance of going in the correct design direction cannot be understated.

Team Dynamics

This design exploration was made up of 4 separate teams exploring different design directions. Each team had 3-4 members and was given some guiding principles to follow. My team of three was tasked to focus on navigation. The drivers of our discovery were:
  • User's control over navigation and content
  • Prioritize timely topics as navigational elements
  • Enable easy video/audio media consumption
  • Incentivize exploration and discovery

Research

Due to this project being an explorative design sprint, we didn't have time to speak to any actual users. Our main methods of research involved the following methods:
  • Competitive Analysis
  • Heuristic Analysis
  • Customer Feedback
  • Personas

Competitive Analysis

I can't disclose all of competitors that we analyzed or the specific findings from those analyses due to confidentiality reasons, but we focused on some of the most popular news media companies and how they utilize navigation to encourage exploration and establish clear wayfinding coupled with intentional personalization. Two competitors that I can disclose are Reddit and YouTube, which are very popular for their ability to encourage exploration through their site, while also giving the users what they initially came for. Overall, we wanted to include the following aspects to stay abreast with current trends and user needs:
  • Exceptional search experiences
  • Sidebar navigation
  • Hyper-personalized content presented in high-priority locations
  • Encourage exploration of various media forms

Heuristic Analysis

WSJ is a behemoth in the digital media space, but its' website definitely has various areas that can be improved. In a quick heuristic analysis using Jakob Nielsen's 10 general principles for interaction design I disovered three primary areas of improvement.
  • Recognition rather than recall
    • It's very difficult to utilize the navigation bar due to a lack of clear and relevant pathways.
  • Aesthetic and minimalist design
    • The current WSJ homepage can appear complex and overwhelming, resulting in cognitive overload.
  • Standards and Consistency
    • Some components on the WSJ homepage are not consistent for various types of content.

Customer Feedback

Audio and video media forms are becoming increasingly popular, espcially among younger audiences. Due to these findings, we knew that prioritizing audio and video consumption was essential. Also, customers want the content to be catered to to them, so we needed to incorporated an exceptional personalization experience.

Personas

WSJ readers come in all shapes and sizes. From experienced financial executives, to middle-class families and novice investors, we had to consider a wide range of archetypes when crafting this experience.

Design

We only had three working days to finish our mockups, so we had to work (very) fast to build out our concepts. The goal was to provide an entire redesign of the WSJ home page that would set the foundation for the rest of the digital experience.

Mood Board

We utilized a mood board to set the foundation of the visual aspects of the design. It was important that we had a clear visual direction for our exploration so that we could have a clear expectation of the visual aspects of the final result.

Parallel Design

First, our team of three went our separate ways to take the guiding design principles and research and begin created. I loved going this route because we were able to diversify our potential options by using divergent thinking. After we created our own wireframes and mockups, we came back together and pointed out the similarities and differences.

Review and Iterate

We turned our rough wireframes and mockups into a polished prototype and went through a series of three reviews with executive leadership members. After three days of hard work, we had two finished protypes, one mobile and one desktop, that we presented to executive design leadership.


My initial wireframe before converging on a design. I utilized icons to encourge navigation and a 'Netflix' style content organization. WSJ would be the first out of its major news competitors to adopt a design like this.

Outcomes and Next Steps

After presenting to design leaadership, our prototypes were then presented to C-suite members to show them the possible directions that WSJ could go in the future. We received very positive feedback and we may see many design elements from our design in a future redesign of one of the world's most trust news sources, the Wall Street Journal.



The design presented is absolutely groundbreaking. Users can freely explore with the convenient sidebar, scroll infinitely either horizontally (in content sections) or vertically and interact with live events and their favorite stocks. This design positions WSJ as not only a traditional news leader, but a futuristic, leading innovator in the media space.
